It has been nearly ten months since I arrived in Buffalo to start my Ph.D. in Geography at the University at Buffalo. Now that the first academic year is wrapping up, I want to write down some honest reflections — on the transition, the work, and what I've learned.

---

## The Transition from Master's to Ph.D.

I completed my M.S. in Geographic Information Science at the University of Zurich, graduating with a *summa cum laude*. That was a good outcome by most measures, and I was proud of it. But a master's program, even a demanding one, has a relatively clear structure: coursework, a thesis, done. A Ph.D. is different. The structure is largely self-imposed. There's no syllabus for the next five years; you build it yourself, week by week.

The first few months were disorienting in ways I hadn't expected. It's not that the work was harder than I anticipated — it's that the feedback loops are longer and more ambiguous. In a master's program, you get signals relatively quickly: grades, thesis defense, advisor comments. In a Ph.D., you might spend months developing a line of thinking before you know whether it's heading somewhere meaningful.

What helped was writing. I started keeping a research journal — not polished, just notes about what I was reading, what confused me, and what seemed potentially interesting. Looking back at those notes from September is strange; I can see how much my thinking has shifted even in ten months.

---

## Teaching as Learning

This semester I served as a Teaching Assistant for GEO 481/506 (Geographical Information Systems Lab). I wasn't sure what to expect from teaching, having been exclusively on the student side for so long.

The biggest surprise was how much it exposed gaps in my own understanding. When you explain something to someone who genuinely doesn't know it yet, you find the places where your mental model is actually a bit fuzzy. I thought I knew how coordinate reference systems worked. Teaching a lab session on spatial joins revealed that I had a few imprecise intuitions I'd been carrying around without examining.

Beyond that, I found that I genuinely liked helping students work through problems. There's something satisfying about watching someone go from confused to competent within a two-hour lab session. I plan to keep TAing — both because it supports the program and because it makes me a better researcher.

---

## Research: Slower Than Expected, More Interesting Than Expected

My research is moving more slowly than I naively projected in September. This is, I'm told, normal. The gap between "I have an interesting idea" and "I have a paper that rigorously tests that idea" is large, and most of the gap is occupied by debugging, reading papers you didn't know existed, discovering that someone already did something you thought was novel, and then figuring out what angle is actually new.

At the same time, the work is more interesting than I expected — mostly because talking to Prof. Hu and engaging with the broader research community has shown me how many interesting questions remain open. I came in thinking I had a pretty good handle on GeoAI as a field. I now think I had a decent handle on a subset of it and was mostly unaware of how large the field actually is.

---

## Buffalo

I moved from Zurich to Buffalo. These are very different cities.

Zurich is compact, organized, and has excellent public transit. Buffalo is sprawling, has a complicated history of urban disinvestment and renewal, and is situated on one of the Great Lakes. The winters are real. In January, I experienced lake-effect snow for the first time, which is a different phenomenon from the snow I knew in Nanjing or Zurich. You walk outside and visibility is forty meters.

What I didn't expect was how much I'd come to appreciate the city. Buffalo has a genuine sense of place — older architecture, a waterfront that's in the process of being reimagined, and a university campus that's large enough to feel like its own community. It's a good place to do the quiet, focused work that a Ph.D. requires.

---

## What Year Two Looks Like

I'm going into the second year with more realistic expectations and, I think, a clearer sense of what I'm actually trying to do. The research directions I'm pursuing — wildfire smoke exposure modeling, informal settlement mapping, disaster impact assessment — are connected by a common thread that I wrote about [elsewhere in this blog]({{ '/blog' | relative_url }}), and I feel more settled in that framing than I did in September.

The goal for year two is to have something to show: a paper submitted, a dataset released, or a method that someone outside my lab finds useful. I'll update this blog when that happens.

For now, the first year is done. On to the next one.
